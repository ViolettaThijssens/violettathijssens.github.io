Airline Booking Process

Quick Overview

Brief

Start-up airline Fly Sun asked to design a booking process, from the homepage up to seat selection, for a new desktop website and mobile app.

Process

My Role

Everything except for building and the final testing stage; when working together in the research stage, I led collaborators through the exercises.

Duration

6 Months (while studying for the UX course that required completing this project).

Success Metrics

Although this was a course project, if this was a real client, I would set goals such as:

  • Sales/customer sufficient to run break-even within three years of operations
  • Customer conversion rate in line with the airline industry
  • Customer net promoter score of 60 (airline industry average is between 38 and 44)

Outputs

Prototypes of the desktop website and a mobile android app, sheet with design elements, and hand-over documentation.

The Project Stages

Strategy

with Word and Photoshop

A company's strategy impacts the design, so I formulated a business and brand strategy for Sun Fly after research on the airline industry.

The results:

  • Sun Fly is tied to a larger corporation that spotted market potential in the European tourism sector after the pandemic
  • The branding of Sun Fly would make a visitor dream of vacation with its playful attitude and focus on customer care

These results impacted the logo, functionalities, visual design and copy. For example, the geographic scope simplified location, language, and currency options.

Competitive Benchmarking

with keynote

I studied airlines inside and outside Europe and a hotel booking site to see what they did well, not so well, and what conventions they used so that I could apply these insights into my designs. I studied airlines popular with tourists, like easyJet and Southwest Airlines, and high-ranking airlines such as Qatar.

Examples of insights:

  • Well done: the search flight menu auto-fills departure airport
  • Not so well done: hard to understand pricing is for all passengers or per passenger
  • Conventions: the seat selection page has a vertical map of the airplane with seats

The insights affected the order and presentation of the content in the booking process.

Usability Testing

with Zoom

I observed how users approached the booking process and their pain points, behaviours, and contexts. Since Fly Sun had no booking process yet, I ran tests on competitor's websites. I moderated one test and took notes on two other tests.

The main insights:

  • Call-to-actions on the buttons so that users know what to do: users sometimes did not know what to do next, for example, how to select a fare type
  • Clear pricing: prices got users confused (totals and prices per passenger or fare type)Â 
  • Functions such as price lock flight, flex option, and save your price did not grab the attention of the participants, you had to prompt them to make them notice

I ensured that elements in my design incorporated the learnings. For example, I created and tested call-to-action labels on all buttons.

Affinity Diagram

with Miro

I used an affinity diagram to organize the research content from the previous stages. I invited a collaborator because it would help me see research materials from different perspectives.

The results were:

  • Groupings of research materials that showed the major aspects of the booking process and their positive and pain points for users
  • The affinity diagram provided groupings that helped construct the customer journey
  • Things that were positive for users, such as a filter on direct flights, were taken into account when designing and pain points, like unclear call-to-actions on buttons, were evaded
  • An understanding of mental models that impacted the design, such as the placements of prices, dates, and times

Customer Journey

with Word and Photoshop

I used the customer journey technique to map the insights from the affinity diagram against the steps in the booking process. I also added quotes from the research materials to make the insights more vivid.

The customer journey map impacted every step of the booking process for Fly Sun. For example, I deduced from the behaviour section in the map that I had to limit the number of choices and enable users to click or tab through the booking process.

Flow Diagram

with Lucid Chart, Word and Photoshop

I created flow diagrams to clarify the steps that the user and the system had to take for completing a booking. I started out with a common use case in Word.

Next, I moved to Lucid chart to build a flow covering all cases — from the happy path to the worst-case scenario. I drew lines in Photoshop in this complex flow chart to highlight specific use cases, e.g., the happy path.

Excerpts from the second flow covering all paths and showing one use case in green:

The main insights:

  • The form on the homepage to select search criteria for a flight should not be treated as linear. Users could fill in the fields in different orders
  • Users had to have edit functionality on every page and had to be able to alter selections on a previous page easily
  • Services and seat selection should not be in the same common region

The flow diagrams gave me a way to follow use cases through the booking process. I could quickly check the flow diagrams to add options for editing selections or build in different paths, e.g., one-way or return trips.

Paper Sketching

with pen and paper

I used paper sketches to iterate quickly through different designs for the booking process. This way, I could pick the best designs.

I attached annotations to the sketches so that others could easily understand why I made certain design choices.

Prototyping

with Sketch, InVision, and Axure

I made initial digital designs and prototypes with Sketch and InVision.

Next, I built prototypes in Axure to make use of Axure's extensive abilities to create interactions.

During the building of the prototypes I made adjustments, such as the colours of buttons.

I ensured that navigation, visual design and typography adhered to accessibility rules. I ran colour checkers on the pages, made sure font size was readable and considered navigation routes with clicking, typing and tabbing. Furthermore, I ensured that elements could be identified in at least two ways, e.g., seating got a colour and icon code.

Usability Testing

with prototypes in a room and online

I organized online and live usability tests to find out positive elements and pain points for users.

The main insights:

  • Add dates when flights were selected so users would know the dates of the flights
  • I made text regarding pricing on every page the same

The usability tests were helpful to get the details of my design right.

Hand-off

with InVision and Axure

Clear communication about the rules and the content of the elements in the booking process to others is important. That's why I paid attention to create hand-off documentation. I made one hand-off in InVision and another in Axure to get to know the possibilities of both platforms.

If the airline had been a real client, I would also have built or contributed to a design library. This would make it easier for the client to expand or change elements in line with previous work.

Learnings

  • Start with the most complex use case first to ensure the elements have enough space to accommodate every case
  • It is important to cover linear and non-linear flows
  • The design changes and improves throughout iterations
  • I learned a million things about UX and UI elements — controls, interaction elements, the importance of motion, hierarchy etc.
  • Studying conventions makes your design good; doing something different and making it better is difficult
  • Material Design and Human Interface Guidelines provide useful material to build out a great user experience
