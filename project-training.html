Training app for Warehouses

Quick Overview

Brief

Current applications do not meet the needs of trainers in warehouses to register training sessions of employees.

Process

My Role

Everything except for building and the final testing stage; when working together in the research stage, I led collaborators through the exercises.

Duration

2 months

Success Metrics

Although this was a project I started because I identified this need in the market, if this was an actual client, I would set goals such as:

  • Registrations are in line with GxP-rules and National Law
  • Trainers spent no more than one hour a week in the application
  • HR sees a reduction of 90% in training and support of trainers to use the application

Outputs

Prototype of the mobile application

The Project Stages

Competitive Benchmarking

with Keynote

I studied a training application at work and some competitors I found on the internet. I learned what they emphasized and their pros and cons, so that I could apply these insights into my designs.

Examples of insights:

  • Market: No training applications tailored for blue-collar workforces
  • Conventions in the market do not work: Training applications for white collars do not work in a blue-collar environment, e.g., blue-collar do not enroll themselves in courses
  • Excel often was used in warehouses and showed big tables with overviews of due dates of training sessions and trainees

The insights affected the application's strategic positioning, which was blue-collars in a warehousing environment. It also had an impact on the application's content (enrollments, courses, learners).

Usability Testing

with Teams

I observed how trainers registered training sessions and their pain points, behaviours, and contexts in an existing application.

The main insights:

  • It was difficult to see who should be enrolled for a training
  • Lots of clicks were needed to get things done
  • Trainers needed to know so many specific terms (due dates, training dates, lessons, courses,...) that they got lost

I ensured that elements in my design incorporated the learnings. For example, I wanted no more than one or two clicks to enroll someone.

Interviews

with Word and Excel

Interviews and an issue list were used to identify conventions, work methods, positive aspects and pain points.

The results were:

  • The number of clicks should be reduced
  • The application should tell trainers what to do instead of them memorizing what to do in the application
  • The number of screens had to be reduced
  • Every choice in the application had to be meaningful

Flow Diagram

with Figma

I created flow diagrams for the actions the trainer had to do. For example, enrollment of an employee.

The main insights:

  • Reduction of the number of clicks
  • While seeking ways to reduce clicking, I realized trainers could handle their training administration much faster on a mobile app than on a kiosk or desktop. For example, a trainer could use a mobile phone or tablet to enter training sessions.
  • Reduction of screens and fields

The flow diagrams gave me a way to follow use cases through the training application.

Paper Sketching

with pen and paper

I used paper sketches to iterate quickly through different designs for the application. This way, I could pick the best designs.

Prototyping

with Figma

I made digital designs with Figma.

The end result was the following prototype: Training Application

You can also watch a demo of the prototype in this video where I give explanations why I made certain design choices.

Usability Testing

with prototypes in a room and online

I organized live usability tests to find out positive elements and pain points for users.

The main insights:

  • I could remove an extra step in the process (clicking an edit button was not needed for clarity)
  • I simplified the actions on the employee screen

The usability tests helped me to improve the design of the application.

Learnings

  • Identify all the workflows (enrollment, course management,...) before design
  • It is important to cover linear and non-linear flows
  • The design changes and improves throughout iterations, testing and user feedback

What I would do differently

  • Take time to visit several warehouses
  • Run more usability tests
  • Do a card sort to identify the most used items (enrollments, courses,...) to put them on the menu bar

Next steps for the project

  • Create hand-over to development
  • Run usability tests before deployment
  • Organize regular meetings between designers and developers to exchange information and review the state of the application
